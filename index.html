What are we looking at?

Children's Anxiety & Race in the United States

Anxiety and depression don't affect all children equally. Across the United States, children from different racial and ethnic backgrounds experience mental health challenges at very different rates — yet these disparities are often invisible in aggregate statistics. This project examines those gaps: who is most affected, where, and why.

Key Indicator

How we measure childhood anxiety

A child is counted as having anxiety or depression if their parent or guardian confirmed that a doctor or healthcare provider has ever diagnosed them with either condition. This applies to children between ages 3 and 17.

Parents were asked: "Has a doctor or other health care provider ever told you that [child] had depression?" or "…that [child] had anxiety problems?" A "yes" to either question qualifies the child for inclusion.

Section Takeaway

The gap between racial groups isn't a snapshot — it's widening. Multiracial children face the highest and fastest-growing anxiety rates, while Asian children report the lowest. These differences suggest that race-neutral mental health policies may not be enough. Targeted, culturally aware interventions are needed for the groups most affected.

Where are anxiety rates highest?

Average Anxiety Rate Across States For Different Races

Looking across all races and all years from 2017 to 2023, a clear geographic divide emerges. Anxiety and depression rates among children range from under 9% to nearly 20%. Maine is the clear high at 19.14%, with Vermont (17.71%) and New Hampshire (17.29%) also notably elevated. On the other end, Hawaii (6.71%) and California (8.43%) are the lowest in the country. The spread is nearly 11 percentage points. The scale of geographic variation suggests that state-level factors meaningfully influence child mental health outcomes.

Do regions tell a clearer story?

Average Anxiety Rate by State and Region

Looking across all races and all years from 2017 to 2023, anxiety rates vary widely by state — but grouping by region sharpens the picture further. The Northeast shows the widest internal spread, ranging from New Jersey (10.14%) all the way to Maine (19.14%), nearly a 9-point gap within a single region. The Midwest is the most tightly clustered, with most states sitting between 11-15%, suggesting more uniform conditions across the region. The South follows a similar pattern — mostly 10-17%, with Georgia (9.86%) as the clear low and West Virginia pulling toward the top. The West is perhaps the most striking: Hawaii (6.71%) and Montana (15.86%) sit in the same region yet are nearly 9 percentage points apart, a reminder that "Western states" can mask very different realities.

Do White and Multiracial children follow the same geographic pattern?

Anxiety Rate By State: Non-Hispanic White vs. Two or More Races

These two groups are selected because they have the broadest and most complete state-level data coverage across years, providing the most reliable basis for geographic comparison.

Side by side, the maps reveal a stark contrast. For Non-Hispanic White children, the pattern tracks the national average — Maine and New Hampshire darkest, the South and West mostly light, California and Hawaii at the low end. The Multiracial map tells a completely different story: Mississippi flips from light to one of the darkest states, and Montana, North Dakota, and Indiana all run noticeably higher than they do for White children. States that look low-risk for one group are not necessarily low-risk for the other.

Section Takeaway

Where a child lives shapes their mental health risk as much as their race — but race and geography interact in complex ways. The same state can be high-risk for one group and low-risk for another, making place-based interventions alone insufficient. A child's zip code and racial identity together paint a far more complete picture of their mental health risk.

Are adverse experiences linked to teen mental health?

Adverse Experiences and Teen Mental Health by Race

This dashboard explores how adverse experiences relate to anxiety and depression among children and teens across racial groups. Here, a child is counted as having experienced adverse experiences if they had two or more of the following: frequent socioeconomic hardship, parental divorce or separation, parental death, parental incarceration, family violence, neighborhood violence, living with someone who was mentally ill or suicidal, living with someone who had a substance abuse problem, or racial bias.

Across the groups with usable data, the relationship is consistently positive states with higher levels of adverse experiences also tend to report higher levels of teen anxiety and depression. This pattern is statistically significant for every group shown in the dashboard, with all reported p-values below 0.05. However, the strength of the relationship differs by race. The Asian group does not have enough reliable data for meaningful interpretation here, so it is excluded from the discussion below.

Section Takeaway

Across the racial groups with sufficient data, higher exposure to adverse experiences is consistently associated with worse teen mental health. The relationship is strongest for Black and Hispanic or Latino children, moderate for Multiracial children, and weaker for White children. This suggests that adverse experiences are an important part of the story behind mental health disparities, but their impact is not equally visible across all groups.
