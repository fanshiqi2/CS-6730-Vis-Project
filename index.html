Mind the Gap

Exploring racial disparities in children's mental health across the United States

↓ Scroll to explore
1

Who is in the data?

The population is far from equal.

Before diving into mental health outcomes, we need to understand who we're looking at. White and Two-or-More-Races groups dominate the sample — meaning some groups lack enough data for deep analysis.

Takeaway Population imbalance means our later correlation analyses will be most reliable for larger groups.
2

Can we trust the data equally?

Record quality varies by race.

Not all survey records are equally usable. Some are flagged as unreliable due to small samples or high variance. The differences across groups are striking.

Takeaway Fewer people → lower-quality records. There's a disparity in the data itself.
3

Where are the gaps geographically?

Valid records differ state by state.

Some states have robust records across all races; others have almost none for minority groups. This reveals which regions we can analyze confidently.

Takeaway Geographic coverage is uneven — future race-specific analyses must account for missing states.

Now that we know who is in the data and how reliable it is, let's see what it tells us about mental health outcomes.

4

Do anxiety rates differ across races?

Average anxiety varies significantly.

Some groups report notably higher rates of childhood anxiety than others. The disparities are clear.

Takeaway Anxiety is not experienced equally — differences in reporting, access, and culture may all play a role.
5

Are things getting better — or worse?

Anxiety trends tell different stories.

Some groups show a clear upward trend year over year; others remain stable. These trajectories guide our deeper analyses.

Takeaway The gap between racial groups may be widening — a signal worth investigating further.
