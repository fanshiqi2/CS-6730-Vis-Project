Introduction

Children's mental health is an important part of overall well-being, yet patterns can differ widely across states and over time. This page highlights three interactive views that focus on Maine, comparing trends in diagnosed depression or anxiety, persistent sadness or hopelessness among high school students, and children's insurance coverage as one possible related factor.

Mental Health

Diagnosed Depression or Anxiety in Children (Ages 3–17)

This view shows that Maine stays at the high end across 2016–2022 for the share of children ages 3–17 diagnosed with depression or anxiety. The pattern reaches its highest point at about 23% in 2021, suggesting that Maine consistently stands out relative to many other states in this period.

Persistent Sadness or Hopelessness in High School Students

In contrast to the first view, Maine remains below the U.S. average across 2009–2023 for persistent sadness or hopelessness among high school students. Maine still rises to around 38%, but that is below the U.S. peak of 42%, showing a different pattern depending on which mental health indicator is examined.

Uninsured Children as One Possible Driver

This view introduces insurance coverage as one possible factor related to children's health outcomes. Maine stays at or below the U.S. level for uninsured children in most years, which suggests that insurance alone may not fully explain the patterns seen in the earlier mental health charts and that additional factors should be explored.
