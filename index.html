Mind the Gap

Exploring racial disparities in children's mental health across the United States

↓ Scroll to explore
1

Who is in the data?

The population is far from equal.

Before diving into mental health outcomes, we need to understand who we're looking at. White and Two-or-More-Races groups dominate the sample — meaning some groups lack enough data for deep analysis.

Takeaway Population imbalance means our later correlation analyses will be most reliable for larger groups.
2

Can we trust the data equally?

Record quality varies by race.

Not all survey records are equally usable. Some are flagged as unreliable due to small samples or high variance. The differences across groups are striking.

Takeaway Fewer people → lower-quality records. There's a disparity in the data itself.
3

Where are the gaps geographically?

Valid records differ state by state.

Some states have robust records across all races; others have almost none for minority groups. This reveals which regions we can analyze confidently.

Takeaway Geographic coverage is uneven — future race-specific analyses must account for missing states.

Now that we know who is in the data and how reliable it is, let's see what it tells us about mental health outcomes.

4

Do anxiety rates differ across races?

Average anxiety varies significantly.

Some groups report notably higher rates of childhood anxiety than others. The disparities are clear.

Takeaway Anxiety is not experienced equally — differences in reporting, access, and culture may all play a role.
5

Are things getting better — or worse?

Anxiety trends tell different stories.

Some groups show a clear upward trend year over year; others remain stable. These trajectories guide our deeper analyses.

Takeaway The gap between racial groups may be widening — a signal worth investigating further.

Now let's see where these rates are highest — and how geography shapes child mental health across the country.

6

Where are anxiety rates highest?

Average Anxiety Rate Across States For All Races & Years

Anxiety and depression rates among children and teens vary considerably across states, ranging from under 9% to just over 20%. Maine is the clear high at 0.2050, with North Dakota (0.1942) and Montana (0.1822) also standing out as notably elevated. On the other end, California (0.0897) and Nevada (0.0941) are the lowest in the country, followed by Texas (0.1021) and Georgia (0.1074). The spread between the highest and lowest states is more than 11 percentage points — a gap wide enough that where a child lives may matter as much as almost any other factor.

Takeaway Geography is a meaningful dimension — Northeastern states lead, Western states lag.
7

Do regions tell a clearer story?

Average Anxiety Rate by State and Region

Grouping by region sharpens the picture. The Northeast has the widest internal spread — New Jersey at 0.115 versus Maine at 0.205 — though most states cluster around 0.13–0.17. The Midwest is similar, with North Dakota (0.194) pulling well ahead of Nebraska (0.118) and Illinois (0.125). The South is the most tightly packed, mostly 0.12–0.15, with Georgia (0.105) as the clear low. The West is the most striking: Montana sits at 0.185 while Hawaii (0.075), California (0.090), and Nevada (0.095) are the three lowest states in the country — a 11-point gap within one region alone. Regional averages point in a general direction, but the outliers raise the question of what else is driving these gaps — which is where race comes in.

Takeaway A clear east-to-west gradient — the Northeast leads while the West lags behind all other regions.
8

Do White and Multiracial children follow the same geographic pattern?

Average Anxiety Rate By State: Non-Hispanic White vs. Non-Hispanic Two or More Races

These two racial groups are selected because they have the broadest and most complete state-level data coverage across years, providing the most reliable basis for geographic comparison.

Comparing Non-Hispanic White and Non-Hispanic Two or More Races children side by side reveals notable differences in geographic distribution. For Non-Hispanic White children, the map tracks the national pattern — Maine and New Hampshire darkest, the South and West mostly green, California and Hawaii at the low end. The multiracial map tells a different story. Mississippi flips from light to one of the darkest states, and Montana, North Dakota, and Indiana all run noticeably higher than they do for White children. States that look low-risk for one group are not necessarily low-risk for the other — race and place interact in ways a single map can't capture.

Takeaway The two groups face distinct geographic risk profiles — Multiracial children don't simply mirror the White pattern.
